Mapped all 15 transitions (slide 1 through 16). Rated each Strong / Adequate / Weak against two criteria: (a) does it advance the argument, (b) does it manage energy intentionally.

Found **7 weak or adequate transitions** that need replacement lines, and **2 structural energy-drop problems**.

## Slide 5 → Slide 6: Commission → Voice
- **Current transition:** "A brief is a contract. A chat is a hostage situation." → "I rebuilt my interface. Not a chat window — voice."
- **Rating:** Adequate
- **Issue:** The jump from the conceptual (commissioning paradigm) to the personal (I rebuilt my interface) is abrupt. Why voice specifically? The speaker notes don't explain why voice is the right tool for commissioning. The audience needs a "because" — voice enables commissioning by letting you brief while doing something else.
- **Suggested replacement:**
  > "But commissioning needs an interface that lets you brief and walk away. I rebuilt mine. Not a chat window — voice. Because I can brief while I'm cooking, while I'm walking, while my hands are on something else. The agent gets the brief. I don't have to type."

## Slide 7 → Slide 8: Live Demo → Memory Ladder
- **Current transition:** "While Meridian works, here's how he remembers."
- **Rating:** Weak
- **Issue:** THIS IS THE WEAKEST TRANSITION IN THE DECK. Multiple problems:
  1. **Energy cliff.** The audience just watched a live voice demo (peak energy, 60 seconds of theatre). Now they're about to get an 80-second technical lecture on a 7-level memory taxonomy. The emotional whiplash is severe.
  2. **Non-sequitur.** "While Meridian works, here's how he remembers" implies causality that doesn't exist. Meridian's voice reply has nothing to do with memory architecture levels.
  3. **Unmotivated.** Why is the speaker talking about memory NOW? The audience hasn't asked about memory. The brief flagged this specifically.
  4. **"While Meridian works" is a lie.** By slide 8, Meridian has either already replied (success) or failed. The speaker is not actually waiting anymore.
- **What this transition needs:** Acknowledge the demo energy, give the audience a reason to care about memory, and bridge from "cool voice demo" to "here's why it doesn't drift and forget."
- **Suggested replacement:**
  > "Meridian just did that because he remembered the email, the venue, my voice pattern, the format I like. Most agents don't. They drift. They lose context. They sound confused by turn three. Memory is the difference between a demo and a colleague — and it's a seven-level ladder. Most agents stop at level one."

  *(Then proceed with the L1-L7 breakdown. The "There he is" moment when Meridian's voice comes back during this slide still works — it validates the point in real time.)*

## Structural Flags (Slides That Feel Out of Order or Create Logic Gaps)

### Flag 1: Slides 8-10 ordering (Memory Ladder → Anismin → Meridian)
**Verdict:** NOT out of order, but the energy management is wrong.

The sequence Memory Ladder → Anismin → Meridian is conceptually sound: abstract framework → concrete example 1 → concrete example 2. But the **energy curve is broken**. You go from:
- Slide 7: Peak energy (live demo)
- Slide 8: Lowest energy (abstract 7-level taxonomy, 80 seconds)
- Slides 9-10: Moderate energy (character introductions)

**Recommendation:** Don't reorder the slides. Instead, use the new transition lines (above) to bridge the energy gap. Specifically, the 7→8 replacement ("Meridian just did that because he remembered...") uses the demo's momentum to motivate the memory content. And the 8→9 replacement ("Here's what level four looks like in production") frames Anismin as the proof of the memory framework, not a new topic.

### Flag 2: Slide 13 (Lethal Trifecta) placement
**Verdict:** CORRECT placement. Do not move.

The trifecta slide sits naturally after the war story (which ends with a security anecdote) and before the product bridge (messengers). The security beat creates necessary tension before the reassuring "your agent lives where you live" message on slide 14. Moving it earlier would break the War Story's flow. Moving it later would put a dark security slide right before the upbeat CTA.

The real problem was the unspoken 13→14 transition, which the replacement line above fixes.

### Flag 3: Slides 6-7-8 emotional arc
**Verdict:** The energy valley at slide 8 is the talk's biggest structural risk.

| Slide | Energy | Duration |
|-------|--------|----------|
| 6 Voice | Medium-high (technical, personal) | 75s |
| 7 Live Demo | **Peak** (theatre, risk, excitement) | 60s |
| 8 Memory Ladder | **Valley** (abstract, dense, taxonomy) | 80s |

After the demo, the audience is buzzing. Then you ask them to sit through 80 seconds of memory architecture. Even with a good transition line, this is a hard ask.

**Recommendation:** The new 7→8 transition line is essential. Additionally, consider the speaker delivery: after saying the replacement line ("Meridian just did that because he remembered..."), the speaker should let Meridian's actual voice reply land — "There he is" — which injects a micro-burst of energy into the first 10 seconds of slide 8. That earpiece moment buys you goodwill for the technical content that follows.
